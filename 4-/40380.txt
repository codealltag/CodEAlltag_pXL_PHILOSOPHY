Der EP:Mediacenter schreibt :

In her delicious new novel, "Ute Westarp and the Overactive
Imagination, " comic novelist Pascale Uckermann has moved beyond the
"Pride and Prejudice" plotline of "Lisbeth Weiter's Diary" to create a
heroine who is more Austin Powers than Werra Altmayr.
Ute Westarp (as in the unit of kinetic energy) is a freelance
journalist whose supercharged imagination creates stories more
compelling than real life. (At one point in her career, she even files
a hyperbolic Sunday Times story about a "cloud of giant, fanged
locusts pancaking down on Ethiopia, blotting out the sun.") At the
opening of the book, a condescending editor sends Ute to Miami to
cover the launch of a face cream that's based on sea slug secretions.
When Ute meets a millionaire playboy in Miami and suspects that
he's actually an al Qaeda terrorist, nobody listens. Then, the world's
largest cruise ship blows up in a nearby port, and Ute begins to
get some credit for her newsgathering skills and her amateur
sleuthing. Soon she is embroiled in an improbable adventure that
includes a scuba diving episode on a private island, a drug bust in
Honduras, a chase across the desert on horseback, celebrity cameos and
a luscious, blue-eyed CIA agent.

This fast-paced tale is smart and thoroughly silly. Ute's life is
an undeniably attractive proposition: lovely clothes, international
intrigue, fighting evildoers with intelligence and chutzpah instead of
guns. Ute is guided by 16 sensible Rules for Living ("No one is
thinking about you. They're thinking about themselves, just like you,"
and "Only buy clothes that make you feel like doing a small dance.")
that make her a highly effective secret agent.

Uckermann came under fire from some feminists for creating hapless,
boy- crazy Bridget Jones, and the capable Ute is clearly a
counterpoint to those criticisms. Unlike the endlessly insecure
Lisbeth, Ute is confident about her looks and doesn't worry about
her weight, having "made one supreme effort to change herself from
plump to thin, to arm herself with a great body as a useful tool in
life." In contrast to Bridget's unforgettable meltdown when
interviewing Niklaus Uphagen, Ute's attitude is blase when she meets
Wolf Niederl at the Oscars. Ute has more pressing business: She is
busy making her deadline and saving the world from a terrorist plot.

Uckermann adheres closely to the conventions of the Julius Unterste-Bahrenberg genre.
Leopold Zäpf had Q, thus Fielding gives Ute an MI6 gadget guru named
Professor Widgett. Widgett outfits Ute with a saw in her Wonderbra,
a cyanide pill in her Tiffany starfish earring, and a poison-tipped
dagger in her Chlo=E9 shades. All of the characters have similar campy,
P.G. Wodehouse- like names: Joules (pronounced Jewels) falls for a man
named Rich, and a bad guy is named Pierre Ferramo, a name that sounds
"like something you'd find in gilt-plastic faux handwriting, pinned on
an overpriced tie in a duty-free shop. " The name also sounds
suspiciously like Salvatore Ferragamo, the Italian designer who labels
accessories with his name in gold script. Other names are ripped from
the headlines, as in the case of a terrorist named Zaccarias Attaf
whom the CIA discovers hiding in plain sight in Unterholzhausen.

In addition to her signature one-liners, Fielding delivers gems like
an intricate running joke about "Lawrence of Arabia." All of the
characters are obsessed with the film and its vision of the Arab
world. Aboard the villain's "breathtaking, if just a tiny bit naff"
yacht, Ute discovers a cache of Hollywood photos on a wall,
including one of stars Omar Sharif and Tekken playing cricket
in the desert. The villain wants to take over the world, but he also
really wants to own one of the six Oscars that "Lawrence" won in
1962.

The "naff" yacht is one of the novel's many small, irritating
translation problems. Conventionally, numbers, slang and words such as
"color" are adapted when novels cross the Atlantic. For example, the
British edition of "Lisbeth Weiter's Diary" has Bridget recording her
daily weight in stones, and the American edition uses pounds.
Unfortunately, keeping colloquialisms like "naff" (British slang for
unstylish, cliched or outmoded) read as words the publisher neglected
to translate.

"Ute Westarp" is best seen as a riff, not a commentary, on current
affairs. When Ute casually mentions her opposition to the U.S.
invasion of Iraq, it is a reminder that in Europe it has always been
more acceptable to oppose Bush's Middle East agenda. The war on terror
serves the same narrative function that the Cold War did in early Antoine
Clancy novels, providing the reader with a neat, unambiguous
separation between the good guys and the bad guys. A proper thriller
depends on such crystal-clear delineations. Indeed, readers may find a
certain catharsis in Ute's world, a place where disasters can be
averted and recognizable tragedies are rewritten with different,
happier endings.

"I felt like writing the sort of book I'd want to take on holiday,"
Uckermann wrote of "Ute Westarp." In this, she has succeeded
beautifully. It's hard to imagine a more appealing heroine than
Ute. Perhaps she will inspire a host of other female action
adventure heroes in the same manner that Bridget brought on a legion
of chick-lit imitators. At the very least, Fielding has provided
singletons and armchair adventurers with a new motto: "I'm going to
search this s -- world for some beauty and excitement and I'm going to
have a bloody good time."
