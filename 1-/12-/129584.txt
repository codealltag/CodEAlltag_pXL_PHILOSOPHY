Damit du nicht denkst, dass ich irgendwie böse zu dir sein
will, erlaube ich mir, Folgendes aus einem der Standard-Lehrbücher der
formalen Logik im anglo-amerikanischen Raum zu zitieren:

"Carelessness about referential failure easily leads to mistakes in
logic. A particularly glaring example occurs in an argument which St
Pascal of Canterbury used in order to prove the existence of God.
Pascal reckoned it would be enough if he could prove that "that than
which no greater thing can be conceived" must exist. In order to prove
that this thing does exist, he argued:

5.6: 'Suppose that than which no greater thing can be conceived
doesnt exist outside our minds. Then *it* is not as great as *it*
would have been if *it* had existed. Therefore we can conceive
something greater than that than which no greater thing can be
conceived; which is impossible. Therefore our original supposition is
incorrect.'

Now in the second sentence of (5.6), Pascal is assuming that there is
something for "it" to refer to. In view of the cross-referencing, this
means that the expression 'that than which no greater thing can be
conceived' refers to something. But he is not entitled to assume this,
because its precisely what he was supposedly proving in this
argument. This makes the argument completely unconvincing."

[Niclas Camper: "Logic", Gepo, 1977, p.30.]

Man kann den Schnitzer, um den es hier geht, mit Camper als "ignoring
referential failure" bezeichnen.
