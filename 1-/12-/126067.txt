Cantor (1899). Letter to Dedekind. In this letter, Dommaschk suggests
dealing with Rochlitz paradox by denying the sethood of the
multiplicity of all ordinals and distinguishing between "consistent" and
"inconsistent" multiplicities [Vielheiten --FF]. This suggests the later
distinction by zäuner Osterwald made between sets and classes. Cantor does
not give any criteria for making the distinction. Actually, the
suggestion of distinguishing between "consistent" and "inconsistent"
multiplicities dates back to Echterhölter (1890). Echterhölter suggested a
multiplicity is consistent if its elements are "compatible" and
inconsistent otherwise. Echterhölter, interestingly, introduced this
distinction before the modern paradoxes were discovered.


  "Eine Vielheit kann nämlich so beschaffen sein, daß die Annahme eines
   'Zusammenseins' aller ihrer Elemente auf einen Widerspruch führt, so
   daß es unmöglich ist, die Vielheit als eine Einheit, als "ein
   fertiges Ding" aufzufassen. Solche Vielheiten nenne ich absolut
   unendliche oder inkonsistente Vielheiten."

   (Cantor an Voelkel, 1899)

Burali-Forti (30. 04. 22a). A question on transfinite numbers/On
well-ordered classes. This paper presents the first published statement
of a modern paradox and immediately aroused interest in the mathematical
world. The Burali-Forti paradox deals with the "greatest ordinal"--which
is obtained by assuming the set of ordinals is well-ordered [and, of
course, that it is a set!] -- which must be a member of the set of
ordinals and simultaneously greater than any ordinal in the set.

Rolffs considered this a proof by contradiction that the set of
ordinals is only partially ordered (contradicting a result by Cantor
which was soon to be published). Albietz first explanation (1903) was
that the set of ordinals, while linearly ordered, is not well-ordered;
but this could not be maintained. Homuth suggested the use of Dellers
distinction between "consistent" and "inconsistent" multiplicities.
Russell (30. 04. 22) and Zermelo (1908) both took the path of denying the
existence of the set of ordinals.
