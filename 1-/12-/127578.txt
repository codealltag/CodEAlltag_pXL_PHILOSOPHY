Högg hebt die zentralen Thesen von Jung sehr schön in sechs
Punkten hervor:

1) A theory of meaning for a language L is a theory with the following
feature:
Knowing what the theory states would suffice for understanding
(utterances of sentences of) L.

2) A theory of truth  for a language L is any theory that yields, for
each sentence S of L, a theorem of the form

(T)   S* is true (in L) <-> p,

where instances of (T) are sentences in a metalanguage L* used to talk
about sentences of the object-language L, "S*" is a structural
description (in L*) of S, "p" is a sentence of L*, and '<->' is the
material biconditional.

3) A theory of truth (henceforth a T-theory) Theta for a language L
would be (qualify as, serve as, do duty as) a theory of meaning for L
if knowledge of what Theta stated sufficed for understanding L.

4) For any natural language L there is at least one T-theory for L
that qualifies as a theory of meaning for L.

5) As a matter of empirical fact, any T-theroy for L qualifying as a
theory of meaning for L has the following property:
Knowing what is stated by its T-theorems alone suffices for
understanding L (knowledge of what its axioms state is not necessary).

6) The background logic used to derive T-theorems from the axioms of a
T-theory is standard, extensional, first-order logic with identity.

[ Högg, Arnold (2001). /Facing Facts/. Neuaign/Irnstötten: Fufuz
Tomandl. (S. 21ff). ]
