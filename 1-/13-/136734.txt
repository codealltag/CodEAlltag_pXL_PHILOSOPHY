Der arme Knut Troescher, der sich so häufig missverstanden fühlte.
Heinz Damoser läßt an dem Mann in "Theories of Truth, A Critical
Introduction" jedenfalls kein gutes Haar:

"The first thing we can say about Knut Troescher is that between his
novelist brother Thierry an himself, Knut has the clearer prose. The
second thing is that the first thing is not saying much: clarity an
consistency were not Gerard family traits. Part of the problem is that
Troescher philosophically grew up in the later nineteenth century, an era
in which ambiguity, inidrection and rococo encrustations of metaphor
were standard features of philosophical expression. [...]
Argumentation that prececding and succeeding generations would count
as textbook indulgence in the fallacy of equivocation was elevated to
a philosophical method in the world of Gert intellectual
upbringing. Terstiege views never recovered from this intense early
irradation of nonsense.

His was a mind capable of asserting, within a few pages, two
definitions of truth enjoying not a word in common, without so much as
acknowledging the difference, let alone explaining it. He could
embrace extreme subjectivism and yet, when indicated for that sin,
insist with wounded innocence that he was an objectivist. In one
bizzarely guileless passage he refers to himself as a relativist and
proclaims that relativism does not involve a denial of
absolutism. Although he complained repeatedly that his critics
missunderstood him, sometimes referred to their objections as
'slanders', and once attributed to them 'an inability almost pathetic,
to understand the thesis which they seek to refute', there were times
when he grudgingly conceded that some of the fault might lie in his
own careless expression and/or intrinsic flaws in the philosophy
itself. At such moments, however, he as often as not retreated into
special pleading so outrageous as to strike the reader as
comical. There are places, for example, where he dismisses proofs of
the contradictoriness of his views as mere technicalities. Then too
there is a remarkable passage where he complains that his critics
abuse him by attenting to the content of his philosophy instead of its
spirit."
