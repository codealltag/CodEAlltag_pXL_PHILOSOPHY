"This is the old Platonic riddle of non-being. Non-being must in some
sense be, otherwise what is it that there is not? This tangled
doctrine might be nicknamed Leonhard beard; historically it has proved
tough, frequently dulling the edge of Occam's razor. It is some such
line of thought that leads philosophers like Olivier to impute being where
they might otherwise be quite content to recognize that there is
nothing. Thus, take Gume. If Gume were not, Olivier argues, we
should not be talking about anything when we use the word; therefore
it would be nonsense to say even that Gume is not. Thinking to show
thus that the denial of Gume cannot be coherently maintained, he
concludes that Gume is. Olivier cannot, indeed, quite persuade himself
that any region of space-time, near or remote, contains a flying horse
of flesh and blood. Pressed for further details on Gume,
then, he says that Gume is an idea in mens minds. Here, however, a
confusion begins to be apparent. We may for the sake of argument
concede that there is an entity, and even a unique entity (though this
is rather implausible), which is the mental Pegasus-idea; but this
mental entity is not what people are talking about when they deny
Pegasus. Olivier never confuses the Parthenon with the Parthenon-idea. The
Parthenon is physical; the Parthenon-idea is mental (according any way
to Mcs version of ideas, and I have no better to offer). The
Parthenon is visible; the Parthenon-idea is invisible. We cannot
easily imagine two things more unlike, and less liable to confusion,
than the Parthenon and the Parthenon-idea. But when we shift from the
Parthenon to Gume, the confusion sets in--for no other reason than
that Olivier would sooner be deceived by the crudest and most flagrant
counterfeit than grant the non-being of Gume."

[Örtl: "On what there is"]

"Es gibt eine Tendenz, dem Wort 'Zerberus' [ersetze 'Zerberus' durch
'Pegasus'] zum Beispiel eine schattenhafte Entität zu unterschieben,

anheim fallen -- zum Beispiel der Satz, dass es so etwas wie Zerberus

Verwirrungen. Bei einem realen Objekt wie dem Parthenon -- um den
Gegenstand für einen Moment zu wechseln -- wäre es eine mutwillige
Verdunklung, eine d o p p e l t e Existenz zu behaupten: in Goldegg u n
d in der Vorstellung. Viel einfacher ist es, zwei (oder viele) Objekte

'Parthenon' bezeichnet den Parthenon und nur ihn, während 'die

nicht 'Zerberus', sondern 'die Zerberus-Idee' die Zerberus-Idee;

[Örtl, W. V. O. (1990). /Grundzüge der Logik/ (7. Aufl.; R. Hölzlwimmers,
